# Player Feedback: The Storyteller

**Date:** 2026-03-11T19:00:00
**Persona:** The Storyteller — narrates everything, names every seed, plays the game as a story unfolding
**Build:** Tick 0–120, CLI play session
**Garden name:** "The Pond of Small Ambitions"

---

## 1. What the game sold me

A cozy ecological sandbox where I could nurture a living world. The manager's brief told me to name my seeds, narrate their stories, and watch them diverge. I came in expecting to feel something.

## 2. What I actually experienced

I planted five named seeds and watched three of them grow into roots while two were accidentally drowned by my own attempt to save them. The game became a genuine tragedy-comedy about a gardener who killed the things they loved most through clumsy overwatering.

**Cast of characters:**
- **Wren** (27,30,16) — planted near the spring. First to root. The golden child. Never struggled.
- **Thistle** (10,10,16) — planted in the dry northwest. Fought for every drop. Got to 72% growth through intermittent water rescues, then was overwritten by the `fill` command I used to save her. Drowned.
- **Fern** (18,20,16) — planted in the middle ground, waiting for the lake to reach her. It never quite did. Got to 25% during brief water contact. Also drowned by `fill`.
- **Rue** (22,25,16) — planted at the water's advancing edge. Quietly grew to root while I wasn't paying attention. The steady one.
- **Lily** (30,28,16) — planted IN the lake. Thrived immediately. Grew to root by tick 120.

## 3. Best moments

- **Wren's first `S`**: Seeing the lowercase `s` become a capital `S` on the map at tick 20. It felt like watching a sprout push through soil. A genuine visual payoff.
- **The accidental drowning**: Realizing that `fill water` over Thistle's position *replaced* her with water. The horror of killing my favorite seed through an act of care. This was the strongest emotional beat of the session — genuinely upsetting and darkly funny.
- **The diverging stories**: Checking `inspect` on Wren (growing happily) vs Thistle (dormant — no water nearby) side by side. The diagnostics created real narrative tension: "she has light but no water, and the lake is so close..."
- **Watching the pond expand**: The water spreading from 16 voxels to 1264 over 120 ticks felt alive. The concentric rings on the ASCII map were beautiful in a minimal way.

## 4. Confusing moments

- **Water spreading but not reaching seeds**: The ASCII map showed `~` water surrounding Thistle and Fern, but inspect said "no water nearby." The visual suggested water was there but the water_level was below 30. This is the #1 storytelling gap — the map lies to you. If I see `~` next to my seed, I expect it to be drinking.
- **Fill overwrites seeds without warning**: `fill water` silently replaced Thistle and Fern. No "this will destroy a seed, are you sure?" There was a "Warning: overwriting..." for `place`, but `fill` just bulldozed everything. This is how the storyteller's garden died.
- **Dormant at 72%**: Thistle grew from 0% to 72% across intermittent water contact, but growth only happens when water is present. The progress bar implies momentum but dormancy is instant and total. It felt like the game forgot Thistle was almost done.

## 5. Boring or frustrating moments

- **Repeated water placement for Thistle**: I placed water near Thistle at tick 20, and by tick 40 the water had spread thin and she was dormant again. I had to keep manually re-watering. There's no way to create a persistent water source — only the spring generates water. This made "rescue the dry seed" feel Sisyphean.
- **No events or transitions narrated**: The tick summary says "Changes: root: +1, seed: -1" but doesn't say WHICH seed became a root. For a storyteller, this is the most important moment and I have to go inspect each one manually to find out.
- **Root is an endpoint, not a beginning**: Once Wren became `*`, nothing else happened. She just... sits there. No further growth, no spreading, no new seeds. The story ends at root. I wanted to see what she'd do next.

## 6. Bugs

### BUG-1: `fill` silently destroys seeds and other placed materials
- **Severity:** major
- **Steps:** Place a seed, then use `fill water` over a region that includes the seed
- **Expected:** Warning that fill will overwrite non-air/non-water materials, or skip seeds
- **Actual:** Seed is silently replaced with water. No warning.
- **Frequency:** 100%
- **Notes:** `place` gives a warning on overwrite, but `fill` does not. At minimum, fill should warn. Ideally, fill should skip seeds/roots.

### BUG-2: ASCII map shows `~` water next to seeds but water_level is below growth threshold
- **Severity:** minor (visual misleading rather than functional bug)
- **Steps:** Let water spread far from source, view map — thin water shows as `~` but inspect shows water_level < 30
- **Expected:** If `~` appears adjacent to a seed, the seed should detect it as water
- **Actual:** Display threshold for `~` is lower than growth threshold (30). Seeds can appear surrounded by water but be dormant.
- **Frequency:** Common at water frontier
- **Notes:** Either raise the display threshold for `~` or lower the growth threshold. The disconnect breaks the storytelling promise.

## 7. Feature or clarity requests

1. **Named tick events**: "Tick 40: Seed at (27,30,16) has taken root!" in the tick summary. This is the single biggest thing that would make the storyteller experience work.
2. **Species/names for seeds**: Even procedural names ("Seed #3" or "Sprout-NW") would help track individuals without manual note-taking.
3. **Fill safety for non-terrain**: `fill` should either skip seeds/roots or prompt before overwriting them.
4. **Water source placement**: Let the player place a persistent spring, not just static water blocks that spread thin and vanish.
5. **Post-root growth**: Roots should do something — spread, produce seeds, absorb water visibly. Right now root is the end of the story.
6. **History on inspect**: "This seed was planted at tick 20. It has been dormant for 15 ticks." would add so much narrative depth.
7. **Water level visibility**: Show a distinction between shallow water (`~`) and deep water (maybe `W` or color difference) so players can read water depth from the map.

## 9. Brutal bottom line: would I come back tomorrow?

**Maybe.** The session genuinely moved me — I cared about Thistle, felt the tragedy of drowning her, enjoyed watching Wren thrive. The inspect diagnostics gave me just enough to narrate ("dormant — no water nearby" is a line of poetry for the right player). But root-as-endpoint killed the story's momentum. Once all seeds are roots, the garden is done. There's no "what happens next." A self-sustaining ecosystem that generates new stories over time — that's what would bring me back every day. Right now it's a one-act play. I want a series.

## Answers to Manager's Specific Questions

**1. Did the game give you enough to narrate? Or was it too mechanical?**
Somewhere in between. The inspect diagnostics are surprisingly evocative — "dormant — no water nearby" told me a whole story. The visual transition s→S→* gave me acts in a narrative. But the tick summaries are too mechanical ("root: +1, seed: -1"), there are no events, and once root is reached there's nothing left to narrate.

**2. Which moment had the strongest emotional beat?**
Accidentally drowning Thistle with the `fill` command. Bar none. It was a genuine gut-punch. The second strongest was seeing Wren become `S` — visual proof that caring for something works.

**3. Did inspect diagnostics feel like a story or a spreadsheet?**
Mostly a story! "dormant — no water nearby" reads like a plant whisperer's diagnosis. The growth percentage and "~30 ticks to root" prediction added suspense. The neighbor water reporting ("neighbor x+1: 255/255") is the one spreadsheet element — I'd rather read "drinking from the spring to the east."

**4. What information would help you tell a better story?**
Event narration in tick summaries ("Seed at (27,30) has taken root!"), seed history ("planted at tick 5, dormant for 15 ticks"), species or auto-names, and some sense of what roots DO after they exist.

**5. Rate the emotional depth 1-5. Would you write a blog post about your garden?**
**3/5.** I would write a short social media post about accidentally drowning Thistle. I wouldn't write a full blog post because the story has one arc (seed→root) and ends abruptly. Give me a second act — roots spreading, new seeds spawning, an ecosystem emerging — and I'd write 2000 words about "The Pond of Small Ambitions" without hesitation.
